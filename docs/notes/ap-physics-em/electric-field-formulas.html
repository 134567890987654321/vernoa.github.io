⚡ Electric Field Formulas

Complete guide to electric field definitions, formulas, parameters, and worked examples.

1. Electric Field Definition

Definition: The electric field is the force per unit charge. It describes the strength and direction of the electric force that would be exerted on a test charge placed at a point in space.

E = F/q

Alternative: E = kQ/r²

Parameters:

E = Electric field strength (N/C or V/m)

F = Electric force on test charge (N)

q = Test charge magnitude (C)

k = Coulomb's constant = 8.99 × 10⁹ N⋅m²/C²

Q = Source charge creating the field (C)

r = Distance from source charge (m)

Example 1: Finding Electric Field from Force

Problem: A test charge of +2 μC experiences a force of 8 N at a point in an electric field. What is the electric field strength at that point?

Solution:
E = F/q = 8 N / (2 × 10⁻⁶ C)
E = 4 × 10⁶ N/C

The electric field strength is 4 × 10⁶ N/C, pointing in the direction of the force (since the test charge is positive).

Example 2: Electric Field from Point Charge

Problem: What is the electric field at a distance of 0.5 m from a +3 μC point charge?

Solution:
E = kQ/r² = (8.99 × 10⁹ N⋅m²/C²)(3 × 10⁻⁶ C) / (0.5 m)²
E = (26.97 × 10³ N⋅m²/C) / 0.25 m²
E = 1.08 × 10⁵ N/C

The electric field is 1.08 × 10⁵ N/C pointing radially outward from the positive charge.

2. Electric Potential Energy

Definition: The work done to assemble a system of charges from infinity.

U = kQ₁Q₂/r
(for two point charges)

Parameters:

U = Electric potential energy (J)

Q₁, Q₂ = Two point charges (C)

r = Distance between charges (m)

U > 0 = Like charges (repulsive)

U < 0 = Unlike charges (attractive)

Example 3: Potential Energy Between Two Charges

Problem: Two charges (+2 μC and -3 μC) are separated by 0.2 m. What is the electric potential energy?

Solution:
U = kQ₁Q₂/r = (8.99 × 10⁹)(2 × 10⁻⁶)(-3 × 10⁻⁶) / 0.2
U = (8.99 × 10⁹)(-6 × 10⁻¹²) / 0.2
U = -0.270 J

The negative value indicates attractive interaction between unlike charges.

3. Electric Potential (Voltage)

Definition: The electric potential energy per unit charge, or the work done per unit charge to move a charge from infinity.

V = U/q = kQ/r
(for point charge)

Parameters:

V = Electric potential (V or J/C)

U = Electric potential energy (J)

q = Test charge (C)

Q = Source charge (C)

Example 4: Electric Potential from Point Charge

Problem: What is the electric potential 0.3 m from a +5 μC charge?

Solution:
V = kQ/r = (8.99 × 10⁹ N⋅m²/C²)(5 × 10⁻⁶ C) / 0.3 m
V = (44.95 × 10³ V⋅m) / 0.3 m
V = 1.50 × 10⁵ V = 150 kV

The electric potential is 150 kV (positive because source charge is positive).

4. Relationship Between E Field and Potential

E = -dV/dr (in general)
E = ΔV/Δx (for uniform field)
E = V/d (uniform field, V = potential difference, d = distance)

Key Insight:

The electric field points in the direction of decreasing potential. The stronger the potential gradient, the stronger the electric field.

Example 5: E Field from Potential Difference

Problem: A uniform electric field exists between two parallel plates separated by 0.02 m with a potential difference of 500 V. What is the electric field magnitude?

Solution:
E = V/d = 500 V / 0.02 m
E = 25,000 N/C = 2.5 × 10⁴ N/C

The electric field is 25 kN/C pointing from high potential to low potential plate.

5. Motion of Charged Particles in Electric Fields

F = qE
a = qE/m
(Using Newton's second law: F = ma)

Parameters:

F = Force on charged particle (N)

q = Particle charge (C)

E = Electric field (N/C)

a = Acceleration of particle (m/s²)

m = Mass of particle (kg)

Example 6: Acceleration of Charged Particle

Problem: An electron (q = -1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C, m = 9.1 × 10⁻³¹ kg) enters a uniform electric field of 5 × 10⁴ N/C. What is the magnitude of its acceleration?

Solution:
a = qE/m = (1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C)(5 × 10⁴ N/C) / (9.1 × 10⁻³¹ kg)
a = (8 × 10⁻¹⁵ N) / (9.1 × 10⁻³¹ kg)
a = 8.8 × 10¹⁵ m/s²

The electron experiences an enormous acceleration due to its tiny mass. The direction is opposite to the field (negative charge).

Quick Reference: Key Formulas

E = F/q = kQ/r²
V = U/q = kQ/r
E = V/d (uniform field)
F = qE
a = qE/m
U = kQ₁Q₂/r
