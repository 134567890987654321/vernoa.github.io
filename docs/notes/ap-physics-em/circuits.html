1. Ohm's Law

Definition: The relationship between voltage, current, and resistance in a circuit element.

V = IR
I = V/R
R = V/I

Parameters:

V = Voltage (potential difference) across element (V)

I = Current through element (A)

R = Resistance of element (Œ©)

Example 1: Finding Current

Problem: A resistor with resistance 100 Œ© has a potential difference of 12 V across it. What is the current?

Solution:
I = V/R = 12 V / 100 Œ©
I = 0.12 A = 120 mA

The current through the resistor is 0.12 A.

2. Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL)

Definition: The sum of all currents entering a junction equals the sum of all currents leaving that junction. Based on conservation of charge.

Œ£I_in = Œ£I_out

(At any node: Œ£ I = 0)

Key Points:

  • Current cannot accumulate at a junction
  • Charge must be conserved
  • Use when analyzing circuits with parallel branches
  • Assign current directions; if negative, current flows opposite direction

Example 2: KCL at a Junction

Problem: At a junction, 3 A enters from the left and 1 A enters from above. How much current must leave through the right branch?

Solution:
I_in = I_out
3 A + 1 A = I_right
I_right = 4 A

4 A must leave through the right branch.

3. Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL)

Definition: The sum of all potential differences around any closed loop in a circuit equals zero. Based on conservation of energy.

Œ£V = 0 (around any closed loop)

Sign Conventions:

  • When moving through a battery from - to + terminal: add the emf
  • When moving through a battery from + to - terminal: subtract the emf
  • When moving through a resistor in direction of current: subtract IR
  • When moving through a resistor against current: add IR

6. Multi-Loop Circuit Analysis (System of Equations)

For complex circuits with multiple loops, use KCL and KVL to set up a system of equations.

Steps:

  1. Label all unknowns (currents in each branch)
  2. Apply KCL at junctions to relate currents
  3. Apply KVL around each independent loop
  4. Solve the system of equations
