✨ Magnetic Induction & Faraday's Law

Complete guide to electromagnetic induction, Faraday's Law, Lenz's Law, and induced currents.

1. Magnetic Flux

Definition: The measure of magnetic field passing through a surface.

Φ = BA cos(θ)

Φ = ∫B·dA (general form)

Parameters:

Φ = Magnetic flux (Wb or T⋅m²)

B = Magnetic field strength (T)

A = Area of surface (m²)

θ = Angle between B and normal to surface

Maximum flux when θ = 0° (field perpendicular to surface): Φ = BA

Zero flux when θ = 90° (field parallel to surface): Φ = 0

Example 1: Magnetic Flux Through a Loop

Problem: A square loop with area 0.04 m² is placed in a uniform magnetic field of 0.5 T. The field makes an angle of 30° with the normal to the loop. What is the magnetic flux?

Solution:
Φ = BA cos(θ)
Φ = (0.5 T)(0.04 m²) cos(30°)
Φ = (0.02)(0.866)
Φ = 0.0173 Wb ≈ 17.3 mWb

The magnetic flux through the loop is about 17.3 millwebers.

2. Faraday's Law of Induction

Definition: A changing magnetic flux through a loop induces an electromotive force (EMF) in the loop.

ε = -dΦ/dt

(The induced EMF equals the negative rate of change of magnetic flux)

Key Points:

  • EMF is induced whenever magnetic flux through the loop changes
  • The negative sign indicates the direction (related to Lenz's Law)
  • Flux can change by: changing B, changing A, or changing angle θ
  • Larger rate of change → Larger induced EMF

For a Coil with N Turns:

ε = -N(dΦ/dt)

Example 2: Induced EMF from Changing Flux

Problem: A circular loop has an area of 0.1 m². The magnetic field through it changes from 0.2 T to 0.8 T in 0.5 seconds. What is the average induced EMF?

Solution:
ε = -dΦ/dt = -A(dB/dt)

ΔB = 0.8 - 0.2 = 0.6 T
Δt = 0.5 s
dB/dt = 0.6 / 0.5 = 1.2 T/s

ε = -(0.1 m²)(1.2 T/s) = -0.12 V

The magnitude of induced EMF is 0.12 V.

Example 3: Coil with Multiple Turns

Problem: A coil with 50 turns, each with area 0.02 m², experiences a magnetic field change from 0 T to 0.4 T in 2 seconds. What is the induced EMF?

Solution:
ε = -N(dΦ/dt) = -N × A × (dB/dt)

ε = -50 × 0.02 × (0.4 / 2)
ε = -50 × 0.02 × 0.2
ε = -0.2 V

The magnitude of induced EMF is 0.2 V (50 times stronger than single loop due to N turns).

3. Lenz's Law

Definition: The direction of the induced current is such that it opposes the change in magnetic flux that produced it.

The induced current creates a magnetic field that OPPOSES the flux change causing it.

How to Apply Lenz's Law:

  1. Identify whether external magnetic flux is increasing or decreasing
  2. Determine the direction of external field
  3. If flux is increasing: induced field opposes (points opposite direction)
  4. If flux is decreasing: induced field reinforces (points same direction)
  5. Use right-hand rule to find current direction from field direction

Example 4: Lenz's Law - Increasing Flux

Problem: A magnet is pushed into a coil of wire. The north pole enters first. Using Lenz's Law, determine the direction of the induced current in the coil.

Solution:
1. The magnetic flux through the coil is INCREASING (magnet entering)
2. External field points OUT of north pole (toward coil)
3. Induced current must create field pointing OPPOSITE (back toward magnet)
4. Induced field points INTO the coil (away from us, if looking at near side)
5. By right-hand rule: current flows CLOCKWISE (when viewed from magnet's perspective)

The induced current creates a magnetic field that repels the incoming magnet (opposing the change).

Example 5: Lenz's Law - Decreasing Flux

Problem: A magnet is pulled away from a coil. Using Lenz's Law, determine the direction of the induced current.

Solution:
1. The magnetic flux through the coil is DECREASING (magnet leaving)
2. External field points OUT of north pole
3. Induced current must create field pointing SAME direction (to replace lost flux)
4. Induced field points OUT of coil
5. By right-hand rule: current flows COUNTERCLOCKWISE (when viewed from magnet)

The induced current creates a magnetic field that attracts the departing magnet (opposing the change).

4. Motional EMF - Moving Through a Magnetic Field

Definition: When a conductor moves through a magnetic field, an EMF is generated across its ends.

ε = BLv

(for conductor moving perpendicular to B field)

Parameters:

ε = Induced EMF (V)

B = Magnetic field strength (T)

L = Length of conductor in field (m)

v = Velocity of conductor (m/s)

Physical Explanation:

  • Moving conductor carries charges through magnetic field
  • Lorentz force separates charges: positive and negative
  • Charge separation creates potential difference (EMF)
  • One end becomes positive, other becomes negative

Example 6: Rod Moving Through Magnetic Field

Problem: A metal rod 0.3 m long moves at 4 m/s perpendicular through a 0.5 T magnetic field. What is the induced EMF across the rod?

Solution:
ε = BLv
ε = (0.5 T)(0.3 m)(4 m/s)
ε = 0.6 V

The induced EMF across the rod is 0.6 V.

Example 7: Current in Closed Loop

Problem: The same rod from Example 6 moves through the field while sliding on parallel conducting rails separated by 0.3 m. If the total resistance of the circuit is 2 Ω, what is the induced current?

Solution:
ε = 0.6 V (from Example 6)
I = ε/R = 0.6 V / 2 Ω
I = 0.3 A

The induced current is 0.3 A flowing through the circuit.

5. Transformers (Using Faraday's Law)

Definition: Transformers use Faraday's Law to transfer electrical energy between coils at different voltage levels.

V_primary / V_secondary = N_primary / N_secondary

V_p / V_s = N_p / N_s

Power Conservation (Ideal Transformer):

I_primary × V_primary = I_secondary × V_secondary

P_in = P_out (no power loss)

Example 8: Step-Down Transformer

Problem: A transformer has 1000 turns on the primary coil and 100 turns on the secondary coil. The primary voltage is 120 V. What is the secondary voltage?

Solution:
V_p / V_s = N_p / N_s
120 / V_s = 1000 / 100
120 / V_s = 10
V_s = 12 V

The secondary voltage is 12 V (stepped down from 120 V).

Example 9: Transformer Current

Problem: If the secondary coil draws 5 A at 12 V, what is the primary current (assuming ideal transformer with 100% efficiency)?

Solution:
P_primary = P_secondary
I_p × V_p = I_s × V_s
I_p × 120 = 5 × 12
I_p × 120 = 60
I_p = 0.5 A

The primary current is 0.5 A (lower current at higher voltage).

6. Self-Inductance

Definition: When current in a coil changes, the coil creates a changing magnetic field that induces an EMF opposing the change (self-induced EMF).

ε = -L(dI/dt)

L = inductance (H or Henrys)
L = μ₀N²A/l (for solenoid)

Example 10: Self-Induced EMF

Problem: A solenoid with inductance 0.5 H experiences a current change from 0 A to 10 A in 0.2 seconds. What is the self-induced EMF?

Solution:
ε = -L(dI/dt)
ε = -(0.5 H)(10 A / 0.2 s)
ε = -(0.5)(50)
ε = -25 V

The magnitude of self-induced EMF is 25 V, opposing the current change.

Quick Reference: Induction Formulas

Φ = BA cos(θ) (magnetic flux)
ε = -dΦ/dt (Faraday's Law)
ε = -N(dΦ/dt) (N turns)
ε = BLv (motional EMF)
V_p/V_s = N_p/N_s (transformers)
ε = -L(dI/dt) (self-inductance)

Key Concepts Summary

Faraday's Law: Changing flux induces EMF
Lenz's Law: Induced current opposes the flux change
Motional EMF: Moving conductor through B field generates EMF
Transformers: Use Faraday's Law to change voltage levels
Self-Inductance: Coil opposes its own current changes
