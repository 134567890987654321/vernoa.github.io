What is the Loanable Funds Market?

The loanable funds market is where savers (suppliers of loanable funds) and borrowers (demanders of loanable funds) interact. The price is the real interest rate, which adjusts to equilibrate supply and demand.

Equilibrium: Quantity of Loanable Funds Supplied = Quantity of Loanable Funds Demanded

Supply of Loanable Funds

Who supplies? Savers, banks, and financial institutions provide loanable funds.

What's in the Supply?

  • Savings by households - People save income for future consumption
  • Business savings - Retained earnings not distributed to shareholders
  • Government savings - Budget surplus (if government revenue > spending)
  • Foreign savings - Capital inflows from abroad (loanable funds from other countries)

Demand for Loanable Funds

Who demands? Businesses, governments, and individuals who want to borrow for investment and spending.

What's in the Demand?

  • Business investment - Firms borrow to buy capital (machines, buildings, etc.)
  • Government borrowing - Government borrows to finance budget deficits and spending
  • Consumer borrowing - Households borrow for mortgages, cars, education
  • Planned investment - The main driver of business demand for loanable funds

Key Variables That Determine Crowding Out

  • Interest rate sensitivity of investment: If businesses are very responsive to interest rate changes, crowding out is larger. If they're unresponsive, crowding out is smaller.
  • Supply of loanable funds: If supply is very elastic (flat supply curve), interest rates won't rise much ‚Üí less crowding out. If supply is inelastic (steep), interest rates rise a lot ‚Üí more crowding out.
  • Magnitude of deficit: Larger deficits shift demand more to the right ‚Üí greater increase in interest rates ‚Üí more crowding out.

Why Crowding Out Matters

  • Limits the effectiveness of expansionary fiscal policy
  • Reduces long-term economic growth (less investment in capital = lower future productivity)
  • Makes government spending less stimulative than simple multiplier models suggest
  • Important consideration for policymakers choosing between fiscal and monetary policy

Important Distinctions

  • Real vs. Nominal Interest Rate: Real interest rate = Nominal rate - Inflation. Use real rate for loanable funds analysis.
  • Money Market vs. Loanable Funds Market: Money market shows how interest rates affect money supply/demand. Loanable funds shows how interest rates affect investment/saving decisions.
  • Stock vs. Flow: Loanable funds is a FLOW (how much is borrowed/saved per period), not a stock.

Quick Facts

  • Market: Loanable Funds
  • Price: Real Interest Rate
  • Equilibrium: S = I (savings = investment)
  • Main users: Savers, Borrowers, Government
  • Key models: Crowding Out, Shifts in S & D
